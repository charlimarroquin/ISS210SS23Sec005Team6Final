Climate Change on Indiginous People

Climate change disproportionately affects indigenous populations and Western discourse about climate change does not address the acute impact on populations living in the arctic/polar regions. This impacts the mental health of the indigenous populations being affected by it, and forces indigenous people to alter their culturally influenced lifestyles and traditions.

About

The circumpolar region of the world, home to a number of indigenous peoples and cultures, is among the most severely impacted by global climate change. The circumpolar region also includes parts of Northern Europe and North America, such as certain Nordic countries, Canada, and Alaska in the US. The concentration of different indigenous peoples populations differs by the area within each country’s borders (pg. 312). The indigenous populations in Northern regions experienced a number of life-altering changes as a result of colonization, subjected to socioeconomic disadvantage, displacement, violence and discrimination, and eventually forced assimilation. In present time, the Indigenous populations in these areas still face inequality, discrimination, and subjugation as a result of their racial and ethnic backgrounds. Despite being forced into assimilation, the indigenous people of the circumpolar region have managed to largely maintain their cultural identity and practices, but they are exponentially threatened by climate change. The spiritual and cultural practices in these populations depend upon the natural resources and biodiversity of the land and sea, but changes in animal behavior patterns, plant growth, and disruptions to our planet’s water cycle, among many other resulting changes from climate change, have devastated the local indigenous communities’ ability to pass down these traditional values and lifestyles to younger generations. In losing access to a climate that facilitates traditional practices, there is an observable impact on the mental and physical health of indigenous population as a result of not only global climate change, but also as a result of the subjugation of indigenous peoples due to a lack of access to mental health services or healthcare targeted at indigenous peoples specific concerns in the midst of worsening climate conditions. These two (however, many more exist that were not discussed in this statement) factors; a particularly sensitive biome and the long term effects of colonization create a perfect storm to alter and devastate the wellbeing of indigenous populations in the circumpolar regions, disproportionately affecting them compared to settlers in these regions or compared to the broader global population of humans impacted by climate change.

Climate change has a greater impact on those of indigenous cultures and in indigenous communities. Indigenous people rely greatly on their land for many resources and climate change alters the way natural resources act and are able to be used. As for indigenous people of high altitude regions, they have an abundance of water for a short period of time with glaciers melting. Indigenous communities in Malaysia are being forced to move for dam construction. Indigenous people have much better knowledge of the land and natural resources than we do. They have lived off of it for many years, therefore they understand the effects of climate change on the natural resources better than anyone. Although the indigenous people’s social, legal, and economic status prevents them from combating climate change and prevents them from protecting their land.
